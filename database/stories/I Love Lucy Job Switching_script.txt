An Original Teleplay by Jess Oppenheimer, Madelyn Pugh & Bob Carroll Jr.

Adaptation by Thomas Wittmer
-------

“JOB SWITCHING”
Adapted from the I Love Lucy episode
Season 2, Episode 1
Original Air Date: September 15, 1952
Original Teleplay by
Jess Oppenheimer, Madelyn Pugh & Bob Carroll Jr.
Adaptation for Modern Single-Camera Production
FULL EPISODE — FINAL DRAFT

COLD OPEN
FADE IN:
INT. RICARDO APARTMENT - LIVING ROOM - EVENING
A fourth-floor walk-up on East 68th Street, 1952. Faded
floral wallpaper. A sofa with cushions pressed into permanent
shapes by the same two people every night. Lamps on, overhead
off. The radiator under the window ticks steadily. Through
the glass — fire escapes, lit windows across the alley, the
faint rumble of the Third Avenue El.
Framed photos crowd every surface: the Ricardos’ wedding day,
Ricky’s orchestra at the Tropicana, a snapshot of the two
couples at Coney Island. A telephone on the side table sits
beside a notepad covered in Lucy’s handwriting — phone
numbers, doodles, half a grocery list.
LUCY RICARDO, early thirties, sits on the sofa in a fitted
periwinkle blouse and charcoal pencil skirt, her red hair
pinned in careful curls. Even at home, even at this hour, she
is fully put together. She is turning the pages of a magazine
she is not reading, her eyes drifting to the front door every
few seconds.
The front door opens. RICKY RICARDO enters — mid-thirties,
dark-haired, Cuban, still wearing the double-breasted navy
suit from his set at the Tropicana, his tie loosened one
notch at the collar. He hangs his coat on the brass hook,
drops his hat on the entryway table where it always goes.
RICKY
Lucy? Is that you, sweetie pie?
He crosses to her, arms wide.
RICKY(CONT’D)
Oh, gee, I’m glad you’re home.
How’s about a great big —
LUCY
(already on her feet)
Well, see you later.
She heads for the kitchen. Not running — walking with
purpose. Ricky catches her arm.
RICKY
Lucy. Come here.
She stops. Turns. She knows that tone.

LUCY
What’s the matter?
RICKY
I stopped by the bank on the way
home and they showed me this.
He pulls a check from his jacket pocket. Holds it up between
two fingers.
LUCY
What’s that?
RICKY
Your check to the beauty parlor.
LUCY
I write a check to the beauty
parlor every month.
RICKY
Yes, I know that. But they don’t
always have a little note on the
back like this one.
He turns the check over. Lucy’s round, looping handwriting
across the back: “Dear teller, be a lamb and don’t put this
through till next month.”
RICKY(CONT’D)
(reading)
“Dear teller, be a lamb and don’t
put this through till next month.”
Now what do you have to say for
yourself?
LUCY
(not missing a beat)
That’s why they call them tellers —
they go around blabbing everything
they know.
Ricky does not laugh. He tucks the check into his pocket and
faces her.
RICKY
Lucy. Now look. I’m serious. I
don’t know what’s the matter with
you. Every month — every single
month — your bank account is
overdrawn. Now what is the reason?
LUCY
You don’t give me enough money.

RICKY
I don’t give you enough money?!
LUCY
Well, we both agree. That must be
the reason.
Ricky’s mouth opens. Nothing comes out.
RICKY
Now look, Lucy —
LUCY
(hands folded)
Yes, sir?
RICKY
Do you think this is a game we’re
playing?
LUCY
No, sir.
RICKY
Do you realize how tough it is for
a guy to make a buck these days?
LUCY
Yes, sir. Yes, sir.
RICKY
Do you think that money grows on
trees?
LUCY
No.
Lucy picks up her magazine. Conversation over.
A KNOCK at the front door — two heavy raps, a landlord’s
knock. Ricky’s face brightens. An exit.
RICKY
(to Lucy, already moving)
Well — we’ll see you later.
He opens the door. FRED MERTZ, early sixties, stands in the
hallway in a baggy cardigan over a wrinkled white shirt, his
thinning hair combed flat across his scalp. Behind him, ETHEL
MERTZ, his wife — a sturdy woman in a floral print housedress
and a sensible cloth coat, clutching her handbag with both
hands.

RICKY(CONT’D)
(grabbing his hat)
Come on, Fred.
LUCY
(from the sofa)
No, no. Come on. Come in and sit
down.
FRED
No, thanks. I went to the fights
last night.
LUCY
Fred, please come in, will you?
Come in, Ethel.
ETHEL
Yeah, please. Please come in.
The Mertzes enter. The door clicks shut behind them. Lucy and
Ethel settle into their respective ends of the sofa. Ethel
slips off her coat, sets her bag on the floor. Fred lowers
himself into the armchair with a grunt and immediately looks
like he’s been sitting there for hours. Ricky, escape
thwarted, puts his hat back down.
RICKY
I — I — Fred, I want to ask your
advice on a certain subject.
FRED
What subject?
RICKY
Well, tell me something, will you?
How often is Ethel’s checking
account overdrawn?
FRED
Never.
RICKY
(stunned)
Never?! How do you manage that?
Fred leans back, fingers laced across his stomach. Ethel
waits.
ETHEL
It’s easy. I never had enough money
at one time to open a checking
account.

Fred’s fingers unlace.
FRED
(sputtering)
What?! She spends money like I was
printing it in the basement.
ETHEL
Printing in the basement. Ha!
FRED
Let’s face it, Rick. When it comes
to money, there are two kinds of
people — the earners and the
spenders. Or as they are more
popularly known, husbands and
wives.
RICKY
Yeah.
FRED
Brother, if they had to make the
dollar, they would think twice
before spending it that fast.
RICKY
Yeah.
Lucy sets her magazine down. Ethel stops fussing with her
coat.
LUCY
What’s so tough about earning a
living?
ETHEL
Yeah.
FRED
Have you ever done it?
A pause.
LUCY
No. But I could.
FRED
(short laugh)
Ha!
ETHEL
I could too.

FRED
(bigger laugh)
Ha!
RICKY
Listen — holding down a job is a
lot more difficult than lying
around the house all day long.
Lucy’s chin drops. That one landed.
LUCY
Lying around the — lying around the
— is that all you think we do?
RICKY
Yeah.
FRED
Well now, let’s be fair, Rick.
Every once in a while, they get up
and play canasta.
Ethel’s eyes narrow. Lucy’s hands go to her hips. She’s done
defending.
LUCY
Who do you think does the
housework? And who do you think
cooks all the meals?
ETHEL
Yeah!
He is pacing now.
RICKY
Oh, anybody can cook and do the
housework.
Lucy sees her opening.
LUCY
Ha! I’d just like to see you two
try it for a week.
A beat.
RICKY
Okay. We will.

FRED
(head snapping toward
Ricky)
We will?
RICKY
Yeah.
Fred’s face cycles through alarm, protest, and surrender.
LUCY
This I’ve got to see.
ETHEL
I want to get a load of —
RICKY
(cutting in)
Yeah, but wait a minute. You will
have to go out and earn the living.
LUCY
Okay. We will.
ETHEL
(head snapping toward
Lucy)
We will?
LUCY
Yeah.
The mirror image. Same alarm on Ethel’s face that was on
Fred’s ten seconds ago. Two marriages. One pattern.
ETHEL
(squaring her shoulders)
We’ll change places. We’ll get
jobs, and you take care of the
house for a week.
LUCY
Okay?
RICKY
Okay.
LUCY
Okay?
FRED
Okay.

LUCY
Okay.
Four people in a living room. Two couples. One bet none of
them can win. Lucy’s hands are on her hips. Ricky’s chin is
lifted. Fred has sunk deeper into the armchair like it’s
already over. Ethel is gripping her handbag strap with both
hands.
Everybody is smiling. Nobody should be.
SMASH CUT TO:
EXT. RICARDO APARTMENT BUILDING - NIGHT
TITLE CARD: “JOB SWITCHING”
The brownstone facade. Fire escapes zigzagging down the
front. The Ricardos’ windows on the fourth floor, warm and
amber. A taxi rolls through the intersection at the end of
the block, taillights smearing red on wet pavement.
We hold on the building for a breath.
FADE TO BLACK.
END OF COLD OPEN
ACT ONE
INT. RICARDO APARTMENT - KITCHEN - MORNING
Morning light through the window over the sink. A small
kitchen — white tile counters, a four-burner gas stove, a
Frigidaire humming in the corner, the checked tablecloth set
for two, a percolator steaming on the counter.
RICKY stands at the stove in Lucy’s apron — a ruffled, floral
thing tied over his undershirt and slacks, the strings
knotted awkwardly at his back. He is whistling. On the table:
eggs, fried potatoes, toast, orange juice, coffee. It looks
perfect.
RICKY
(calling out)
Lucy! Breakfast is ready.
LUCY
(from the other room)
Coming, dear.

LUCY enters in a belted olive day dress and low heels, a
string of pearls at her throat — dressed to make an
impression at whatever employment office will have her. She
stops in the doorway. Takes in the spread.
LUCY(CONT’D)
Well, doesn’t this look wonderful.
Did you have any trouble?
RICKY
No. Not a bit.
LUCY
Well — happy housework.
RICKY
Happy job hunting.
LUCY
Thank you.
She sits. Unfolds her napkin. Takes a bite of the eggs. Then
the potatoes. Then a sip of coffee.
LUCY(CONT’D)
Oh, boy, does this look good. Mmm.
I’m hungry this morning too.
Another bite. She chews. Nods.
LUCY(CONT’D)
Mmm. It is good.
RICKY
(leaning against the
counter, arms crossed)
Why, naturally. There’s nothing to
cooking.
LUCY
Mmm. Oh, the coffee’s good too.
Where did you learn to do all this?
RICKY
Oh, I’m just a natural-born cook, I
guess.
Lucy eats. Ricky watches her eat.
RICKY(CONT’D)
Honey, what kind of a job are you
going to get? Are you going to go
to an employment office?

Lucy eats. Does not look up.
RICKY(CONT’D)
Lucy, why don’t you answer me?
LUCY
(mouth full, not looking
up)
That’s nice, dear.
Ricky stares at her.
LUCY(CONT’D)
(swallowing, looking up)
Okay, I get your message. Now you
know what I go through every
morning.
RICKY
All right. I’ll reform.
LUCY
Oh. Gee, these fried potatoes are
just great.
RICKY
I don’t see why you’re so
surprised. There’s nothing to it.
The TELEPHONE rings in the living room. Ricky unties the
apron and goes to answer it. Lucy keeps eating.
INT. RICARDO APARTMENT - LIVING ROOM - CONTINUOUS
Ricky picks up the receiver. Through the kitchen doorway,
Lucy is still working on the potatoes.
RICKY
Hello? Yes? Oh, yes. Oh. Well,
thank you very much. Yes. Good-bye.
He hangs up. Stands there a moment. Does not move back toward
the kitchen.
LUCY
(calling from the kitchen)
Who was that? Gee, this is a
sensational breakfast. My gosh, but
you’re a good cook.
Ricky walks back to the kitchen doorway. Slowly.

INT. RICARDO APARTMENT - KITCHEN - CONTINUOUS
Lucy is buttering toast. She looks up.
LUCY
Who was that on the phone?
RICKY
It was the drugstore.
Lucy stops buttering.
LUCY
The drugstore?
RICKY
I left my hat.
LUCY
When you picked up these two
breakfast orders.
Not a question. She has put it together. Ricky does not deny
it.
LUCY(CONT’D)
You know, you could have tried to
cook this yourself. We have a dozen
eggs in the refrigerator.
RICKY
Well, um — they didn’t turn out
quite right.
LUCY
The whole dozen?!
RICKY
Yeah.
Lucy looks at him. Ricky looks at the beautiful breakfast he
did not make.
LUCY
Oh.
A KNOCK at the kitchen door. ETHEL pushes in — wool coat over
a navy dress, a small hat pinned at an angle, her good
handbag. Dressed for the outside world.
ETHEL
Morning, everybody.

LUCY
Hi, Ethel.
RICKY
Good morning.
ETHEL
Well, Lucy, are you ready to go to
the office? To the employment
office, that is.
LUCY
Yeah. All ready.
Lucy stands, brushes the crumbs from her lap, picks up her
handbag and gloves from the counter.
LUCY(CONT’D)
Okay, dear. Bye-bye.
RICKY
Bye.
ETHEL
Bye, Ricky.
RICKY
Bye. See you for dinner.
Lucy and Ethel head for the front door. From upstairs,
through the ceiling, FRED’S VOICE:
FRED (O.S.)
Ethel! Ethel, you’re leaving.
ETHEL
(not stopping)
Yeah? What about it?
FRED (O.S.)
Well, you forgot to kiss me good-
bye.
Ethel does not go back upstairs. Lucy takes her arm and they
walk out. The door closes behind them.
Ricky stands alone in the kitchen. The apron is folded over a
chair. The beautiful breakfast sits on the table, half-eaten,
fully fraudulent.
CUT TO:

EXT. EAST 68TH STREET - MORNING
Lucy and Ethel on the sidewalk, walking east, shoulder to
shoulder. Morning sun on the brownstones. A delivery truck
rumbles past. Lucy’s heels click on the pavement. Neither
woman says a word — the confidence is in the walk.
CUT TO:
INT. EMPLOYMENT AGENCY - WAITING AREA - MORNING
A row of wooden chairs along an institutional green wall,
fluorescent lights, a frosted glass window with gold
lettering reading EMPLOYMENT SERVICES. The room smells like
floor wax and other people’s cigarettes.
Lucy and Ethel sit side by side, handbags in their laps —
Lucy sitting up straight, performing readiness, Ethel
gripping the clasp of her bag.
ETHEL
(leaning in)
Lucy, I’m nervous.
LUCY
So am I.
ETHEL
Do you think this man can really
get jobs for us?
LUCY
Sure he can.
ETHEL
But we don’t know how to do
anything.
LUCY
Shh!
Across the room, a MAN rises from a desk, shakes hands with a
young woman in a typist’s blouse, and hands her a slip of
paper.
SNODGRASS
Here’s the address. Report for work
first thing in the morning.
The woman leaves. SNODGRASS looks down at his appointment
sheet.

SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Who’s next?
LUCY
(jumping up)
We are! We are!
ETHEL
(jumping up with her)
We’re together.
INT. EMPLOYMENT AGENCY - SNODGRASS’S DESK - CONTINUOUS
SNODGRASS — fiftyish, wire-rimmed glasses, white shirt with
the sleeves rolled, a striped tie pulled loose. A patient
man. Not infinitely patient. On the desk, a blotter, a pen
set, and a metal box of index cards.
Lucy and Ethel settle into the two chairs facing the desk.
Lucy crosses her ankles. Ethel puts her handbag on her knees
like a shield.
SNODGRASS
I see. Well, what job did you have
in mind?
LUCY
Uh, what kind of jobs do you have
open?
SNODGRASS
Well, what do you do?
LUCY
What kind of jobs do you have open?
SNODGRASS
Well, what do you do?
LUCY
What kind of jobs do you have open?
Snodgrass looks from Lucy to Ethel and back.
SNODGRASS
You go first this time.
LUCY
All right.
SNODGRASS
What do you do?

LUCY
What kind of jobs do you have open?
SNODGRASS
No! Cut that out! Please tell me,
what do you do? Are you
stenographers?
Lucy grabs it.
LUCY
Oh, yes, that’s it. We’re
stenographers.
SNODGRASS
Well, why didn’t you say so? I have
a lot of stenographic jobs
available.
LUCY
Oh, good.
SNODGRASS
How fast can you type?
Lucy holds her hands up and wiggles her fingers in the air —
a pantomime of typing.
LUCY
Oh, about this fast.
Snodgrass watches the fingers. Looks down at his notepad.
SNODGRASS
Oh, I see. And how about your
typing?
Under the desk, Lucy nudges Ethel’s foot.
ETHEL
Uh, mine’s not as good as hers.
SNODGRASS
No.
Snodgrass sets his pen down.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Look, ladies, let’s be honest with
one another. I can’t find you a job
until you can tell me what you can
really do. Now — what do you do?

LUCY
What kind of jobs do you have open?
Snodgrass closes his eyes. Opens them.
SNODGRASS
All right. Let’s try it another
way. I have a list. I will read off
the jobs that I have available, and
you tell me if you’ve had any
experience in that line.
LUCY
Oh, fine.
He opens the metal box and pulls out a stack of index cards.
Reads the first one.
SNODGRASS
Bookkeeper.
Lucy and Ethel shake their heads.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Comptometer operator.
They shake their heads.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Dental technician.
They shake their heads.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Insurance adjuster.
They shake their heads. The hope is leaving their faces.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Registered nurse.
They shake their heads. Slower this time. Ethel glances at
Lucy.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
P.B.X. operator.
They shake their heads and look at each other. A little wild-
eyed.
SNODGRASS(CONT’D)
Well, I’ve only got one left. Candy-
makers. Have you ever had any
experience making candy?

Lucy does not hesitate.
LUCY
What luck — you just hit our
specialty! I don’t know why we
didn’t tell you before.
SNODGRASS
You’re candy-makers?
ETHEL
Oh, yes. We’ve made a lot of candy.
Snodgrass looks at them. He decides not to investigate.
SNODGRASS
You can begin work today at
Kramer’s Kandy Kitchen.
LUCY
Oh, thank you.
ETHEL
Thank you.
Lucy and Ethel stand. Handshakes. Smiles. They walk out of
the office with the bearing of two women who have just solved
a problem that has not yet begun.
CUT TO:
EXT. EMPLOYMENT AGENCY - MORNING
Lucy and Ethel on the sidewalk. Bright sun. Lucy adjusts her
gloves. Ethel holds the address slip.
ETHEL
Candy-makers?
LUCY
How hard can it be?
They walk. Heels clicking. Headed east.
CUT TO:
INT. RICARDO APARTMENT - LIVING ROOM - DAY
The ironing board is set up where the coffee table usually
goes. A basket of wrinkled laundry sits on the sofa, a can of
spray starch on the side table next to the candy dish.

RICKY stands at the ironing board in an undershirt and
slacks, a dish towel thrown over one shoulder. He is pressing
a blouse with the intense focus of a man defusing a bomb. A
thin curl of smoke rises from the fabric.
He does not notice the smoke. A KNOCK at the door. He sets
the iron down and opens it.
FRED stands in the hallway in a moth-eaten undershirt and old
trousers held up by suspenders, a kerchief knotted over his
head. He looks like he has been in a fight he did not start.
RICKY
(whistling)
Oh, hiya, Fred.
FRED
Hi, Rick.
RICKY
Well, don’t you look cute. What are
you doing with that scarf on top of
your head?
FRED
Oh! Oh, you mean this. I was
cleaning the apartment and I didn’t
want to get any dust in my hair.
Ricky nods. Reasonable.
RICKY
Hey, take a tip from me — from an
old housewife like me, will ya —
and don’t let that iron get too
hot. You might scorch something.
FRED
Really?
RICKY
Yeah.
Fred glances at the ironing board. At the blouse. At the
brown mark spreading across the collar.
FRED
You mean, uh — like this?
Ricky looks down. Lifts the iron. The scorch is unmistakable.
RICKY
That’s the, uh — that’s the general
idea, yes.

RICKY(CONT’D)
Well, I thought I’d tell Lucy that
this was, uh, an original new
design.
FRED
That story won’t work.
RICKY
No, eh? Why not?
FRED
Ethel has three blouses with that
same design.
They look at the scorch mark. Two men, same problem, same
failed solution.
FRED(CONT’D)
Listen, you know what’s really
tough? Silk stockings.
RICKY
Yeah, how about that?
FRED
They’re really tough to iron.
RICKY
You’re not supposed to iron silk
stockings.
FRED
No?
RICKY
No. Fred, anybody knows that.
FRED
Look, just make sure that you use
plenty of starch when you wash
them.
RICKY
Oh. I see. I’ll show you. I got a
couple of them over here.
Ricky picks up a pair of stockings from the laundry basket.
Holds them up. They are rigid — starched stiff, pressed with
creases sharp enough to cut paper. They do not drape. They
stand.
RICKY(CONT’D)
Here. See?

FRED
Oh, beautiful.
RICKY
Now, you seen better creases than
that anyplace?
FRED
My boy, you’re a regular Margaret
McBride.
RICKY
Why, sure, man, sure.
They admire the stockings. Two men who have no idea what
they’re looking at.
RICKY(CONT’D)
Oh, you know something? I been
thinking about that dinner, you
know?
FRED
Yeah?
RICKY
Yeah. I think it’s silly for both
of us to cook a whole meal, you
know? So I thought what we should
do is pool our talents — I’ll cook
the main course and you cook the
dessert.
FRED
Okay.
RICKY
Now let’s see. How about arroz con
pollo?
FRED
Well, which is that — the main
course or the dessert?
RICKY
Chicken and rice.
FRED
Oh-ho! I seen my mother do it a
hundred times.
RICKY
I love it.

RICKY(CONT’D)
Well listen, I tell you what. Can
you bake a cake for dessert?
FRED
Will I — a big juicy one, seven
layers thick.
RICKY
Yeah? With frosting?
FRED
Globs of frosting.
Both men chuckle.
RICKY
Boy, this is going to be the best
meal our wives ever had.
They stand in the living room — scorched blouse on the
ironing board, rigid stockings in hand, the dinner plan
settled. Complete confidence. Zero ability.
END OF ACT ONE
ACT TWO
CUT TO:
EXT. KRAMER’S KANDY KITCHEN - DAY
A squat brick building on a commercial block. A painted sign
over the entrance: KRAMER’S KANDY KITCHEN. Women in white
smocks file through the front door. Lucy and Ethel approach
from the sidewalk, address slip in hand.
CUT TO:
INT. KRAMER’S KANDY KITCHEN - DIPPING ROOM - DAY
A long marble slab runs the length of the room — at one
station, a big puddle of melted chocolate with cream centers
in neat rows; beside it, an empty station with its own
puddle, untouched, waiting. The air is thick and sweet.
A SKILLED WORKER sits at the first station — middle-aged,
quick hands, white smock. She picks up a cream center, rolls
it deftly in the chocolate, coats it evenly, sets it on the
other side, and with the excess on her fingers makes a small
decorative swirl on top. She does this the way a pianist
plays scales. Without thinking.

LUCY and ETHEL — in smock-type aprons and candy kitchen caps
— walk in behind the FORELADY, a tall, broad-shouldered woman
in a starched white supervisor’s coat, her hair pinned under
a hairnet, a clipboard against her ribs like a weapon. She
does not smile. It is possible she has never smiled.
FORELADY
All right, girls. This is the first
stop. Now you understand from your
indoctrination session — you’re
here for one purpose. To work.
LUCY
Yes, ma’am.
FORELADY
Ricardo, I’m going to put you to
work chocolate dipping. You say
you’ve had experience.
LUCY
Oh, yes, ma’am. I’m a dipper from
way back. They used to call me the
big dipper.
The Forelady does not blink.
FORELADY
There’s no room in this plant for
levity, however weak.
LUCY
(smile vanishing)
Yes, ma’am.
FORELADY
All right, Ricardo. That’s your
place.
She points to the empty station beside the skilled worker.
Lucy sits. The marble slab stretches in front of her — the
chocolate puddle, the cream centers, the same setup as the
woman beside her.
FORELADY(CONT’D)
Mertz, I’m going to put you to work
down in the chocolate boxing
department. Follow me.
ETHEL
Yes, ma’am.

Ethel follows the Forelady out, glancing back. Lucy gives a
small wave with chocolate-free fingers. It is the last time
her fingers will be chocolate-free today.
Lucy watches the woman beside her. Pick up, roll, coat, set,
swirl. Pick up, roll, coat, set, swirl. It looks easy.
LUCY
(to herself)
Hey, this is fun.
She reaches for a cream center. Gets it on the second try —
the first try it shoots out of her fingers like a wet seed.
She dips it in the chocolate. Rolls it. The coating is thick
on one side, bare on the other. She dips again. Now it is the
size of a golf ball.
She sets it on the slab and attempts the decorative swirl on
top. The result does not resemble a swirl. It resembles a
fingerprint at a crime scene.
She glances at the skilled worker’s row of finished
chocolates: uniform, glossy, each one topped with an
identical flourish. She looks at her own: three lumps of
varying size, none of them symmetrical.
She tries another. Worse.
Her nose itches. Both hands are covered in chocolate to the
wrist.
LUCY(CONT’D)
(to the skilled worker)
What do you do when your nose
itches?
